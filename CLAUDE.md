# CPSC 430 — Computers and Society (UBC, 2026 Summer 2)

Personal coursework directory for an ethics/writing course on the social and ethical
implications of computing. This is **not a code project** — the work here is *writing*
(argumentative mini-essays), reading, and lecture note-taking. There is nothing to build,
run, or test.

## Layout

| Directory | Contents |
|---|---|
| `admin/` | Syllabus and course logistics (`syllabus.pdf`) |
| `lecture/` | Lecture notes in Markdown, `lecture-N.md` |
| `essays/` | One folder per mini-essay: `e1/`, `e2/`, … |
| `reading-materials/` | Course textbook (`textbook.pdf` — Quinn, *Ethics for the Information Age*) |
| `slides/` | Lecture slide decks (`.pptx`) |
| `quizzes/` | Reading-quiz material (currently empty) |

## Essay folders (`essays/eN/`)

Each essay gets its own folder. Typical contents:
- `prompt.md` — the assignment prompt (choose one of N sub-prompts to argue for/against).
- `draft.md` — the student's working draft (may be empty until started).
- Source articles saved as Markdown (e.g. `a1-*.md`, `a2-*.md`) when the prompt is
  reacting to specific readings.

Essay prompts are **argumentative**: pick a position and defend it. Prompts reference
textbook sections (e.g. "Secs. 2.1–2.5", ethical egoism, Quinn's standard for evaluating
ethical theories).

## Course mechanics (from `lecture/lecture-1.md`)

- **10 mini-essays total, ~2 per week**; essay weight increases over the term.
- Essays are written in the **CBTF** (Computer-Based Testing Facility). A ~150-character
  outline is drafted in **MTA** beforehand and carries into the CBTF.
- **Peer review** is central: ≥3 peer reviews per essay; a TA's grade overrides peer
  grades; grades can be appealed (TA regrade is final).
- Must complete the **reading quiz** before doing peer reviews.
- **Calibrated reviews** (grading pre-scored essays) build a "dependability score."

## Peer-review rubric

Essays are scored on four criteria, each **0–5** (0 = no text / non-English / outline-only
submission). Use these when drafting or self-assessing an essay.

1. **WELL STRUCTURED** — states a relevant thesis, supports it with argument(s) clearly
   related to the point and (if relevant) distinct from one another, linked logically.
   - 1: arguable but no essay format; progression unclear/unrelated.
   - 2: thesis stated but struggles with essay format; some content irrelevant/redundant.
   - 3: thesis relevant, somewhat standard format, but arguments/counterarguments
     insufficiently distinct and/or poorly linked.
   - 4: all args/counterargs support a clear, relevant thesis in standard format; distinct
     and logically progressing.
   - 5: as 4, but well-structured with a strong progression that excellently supports the thesis.

2. **GOOD JOB OF MAKING ITS CASE** — relevant arguments, backed with evidence/examples at an
   appropriate level of detail, responding to contrary views.
   - 1: only irrelevant information.
   - 2: many facts, but arguments/evidence/opinions indistinguishable.
   - 3: recognizable arguments with evidence, but relevance/detail very inappropriate; no
     counterarguments addressed.
   - 4: clear arguments generally supporting the thesis, generally relevant evidence, some
     attempt at counterarguments.
   - 5: compelling case (agree or not); very relevant arguments, appropriate evidence, strong
     responses to counterarguments.

3. **UNDERSTANDING OF SUBJECT MATTER** — course topic and wider context (frameworks, theories,
   evidence).
   - 1: no relevant course material.
   - 2: some relevant material but many errors/inaccuracies; opinion presented as fact.
   - 3: mostly relevant but some errors or omissions; opinion may be presented as fact.
   - 4: relevant material, generally correct/reasonable understanding, no significant errors.
   - 5: broadens scope with creative, insightful understanding and balanced use of course material.

4. **CLEAR AND IN CORRECT ENGLISH**
   - 1: nearly indecipherable.
   - 2: decipherable only with significant effort; many errors; language may be inappropriate.
   - 3: requires some work to understand; some errors; possibly inappropriate language.
   - 4: communicates ideas well; a few errors that mildly affect flow but not understanding.
   - 5: clear and concise; minimal, easily-ignored errors.

Note: an outline-only submission scores **0** on every criterion — the essay must be expanded
into full prose to be graded.

## Note-taking conventions (`lecture/lecture-N.md`)

Lecture notes follow a consistent structure worth preserving when editing/adding:
- `### General Notes` — logistics and content
- `### Small Group Discussion` — in-class debate positions and supporting points
- `### Questions` — `Q:` / `A:` pairs; some answers are left blank to fill in later
- `### Action Items` — deadlines and to-dos

## Working in this directory

- When asked to help with an essay, work in the relevant `essays/eN/draft.md`, following
  the position/sub-prompt in that folder's `prompt.md`.
- PDFs and `.pptx` are binary; use the Read tool's `pages` parameter for the textbook PDF.
- This is graded academic writing — help with structure, argument, and clarity, but the
  student's own reasoning and voice should drive the essays.
