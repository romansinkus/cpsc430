A defendant can now be jailed before trial not because a judge weighed their case, but because an algorithm assigned them a number. Using AI to make bail decisions is harmful because these tools inherit bias from the data they are trained on and hide that bias behind an unauditable algorithm.

Bail-risk tools are trained on historical arrest and criminal-justice data that reflects decades of biased policing. On the surface, a risk score looks objective — a neutral number produced by a machine rather than a fallible human judge. But that objectivity is an illusion. Because the training data already encodes racial and economic inequity, the model reproduces and amplifies it, laundering old discrimination into new math. COMPAS, a risk-assessment tool used in bail and sentencing, illustrates this: an investigation found it flagged Black defendants as high-risk at roughly twice the rate of white defendants who never reoffended. The "objective" system was, in practice, harsher on groups already historically discriminated against.

A defender might respond that this is fixable: if the model is biased, we can simply correct it and make it fair. But you cannot correct a bias you cannot see. These systems are opaque — COMPAS by trade secret, since the company that built it refuses to disclose how it works, and newer neural-network models by their very design, since not even their creators can fully explain a given decision. If the reasoning behind a score cannot be inspected, the bias inside it cannot be audited or fixed. "We can just correct it" assumes access to the model that no one — not defendants, not lawyers, not judges — actually has.

This is what makes AI bail decisions uniquely harmful. A biased human judge can at least be questioned and appealed; an opaque algorithm cannot. Deferring to one does not remove bias from the courtroom — it replaces a judge who can be challenged with a mathematical authority that cannot. When a person's freedom before trial, while still legally innocent, rests on a score no one can explain, we have not made the system fairer. We have only made its unfairness harder to see.
