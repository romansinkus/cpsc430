It is not uncommon for humans to act selfishly. Along with other instincts like cooperation, acting in one's self-interest is quite natural. However, it is not most natural for humans to act in their own self-interest due to instinctive altruistic behaviors and self-sabotage.

Humans often find themselves acting altruistically towards strangers without expecting to receive an award or benefit. Warneken and Tomasello (2006) discovered that children as young as 18 months old spontaneously helped strangers retrieve out-of-reach objects without being asked or rewarded. This shows that babies indicate clear signs of altruism before socialization has instilled any values on them, making this truly a natural action rather than a characteristic learned by others. If it was most natural for the babies to act in their own self-interest, during this study they would have not offered a helping hand in order to save themselves the energy. Therefore despite the option to act in one's self-interest, people often find themselves helping those around them. Even mature adults exhibit this same altruistic behaviour as they care for their children. Parents often sacrifice their own well-being for the benefit of their children.

One may argue that all examples of true altruism are disguised selfless actions made to maximize long-term well-being. In the case of the baby, one could argue that they've instinctively done this assuming that this will help them reap some sort of benefit from the adult later on like food, shelter, or protection. In the case of the parent, one could argue that they protect their child to prevent the feeling of guilt. There all examples of altruism are considered to be selfish, then there is no notion of selfish vs. unselfish if they are all classified as selfish.

Beliefs like ethical egoism are tempting to believe since we often find ourselves prioritizing our needs over others, however this line of thought naturally breaks down as the belief is universalized to all situations. Consequently, acting in one's self interest is often a natural instinct for humans, however it is not the most natural due to other instincts often taking precedence in different scenarios.

### Polished Draft (2559 chars, slightly over 2500)

Proponents of ethical egoism often hold that it is most natural for people to act in their own self-interest. Self-interested behaviour is certainly natural — alongside instincts like cooperation, we act on it constantly. But it is not the most natural or dominant driver of human action: we frequently act for the benefit of others, and just as frequently fail to act in our own interest at all.

Humans often find themselves acting altruistically towards strangers without expecting any reward or benefit. Warneken and Tomasello (2006) discovered that children as young as 18 months old spontaneously helped strangers retrieve out-of-reach objects without being asked or rewarded. This shows clear signs of altruism before socialization could have instilled such values, making it a genuinely natural action rather than a learned one. If it were most natural for the babies to act in their own self-interest, they would have withheld their help to conserve their own energy. This same instinct appears in its most extreme form among adults: parents across nearly every culture routinely sacrifice their own well-being so that their children can survive.

One may argue that all of these acts are simply disguised self-interest. Even the child, one could say, enjoys the warm feeling of helping, and the parent sacrifices only to spare themselves the pain of guilt — so beneath every apparent kindness the actor is really paying themselves back, materially or emotionally. This objection assumes people consistently maximize their own well-being — yet they knowingly act against it all the time. Many students procrastinate on studying they know is important, gaining no real enjoyment from the avoidance — only stress and guilt — while sabotaging the grades that would open doors to jobs, scholarships, and graduate school. Such behaviour serves neither their long-term nor their immediate interest, offering not even the fleeting pleasure the objection would predict. The theory cannot cast us as calculating and self-serving whenever it suits its reinterpretation of our kindness, then overlook how routinely we work against our own good.

The premise that acting in one's self-interest is most natural is tempting, since we do often place our own needs first. But it cannot account for the full range of our behaviour: we help strangers with nothing to gain, and we routinely undermine our own well-being for no benefit at all. Acting in one's self-interest is one natural human instinct, but it is not the most natural or dominant driver of what we do.
