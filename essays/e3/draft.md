Rule utilitarianism is an ethical framework that creates and evaluates general rules based on their net positive or negative impact. According to rule utilitarianism, a student exploiting a security flaw to modify their own grades is morally unjustifiable.

The general rule would permit grade-exploitation, which would strip the integrity of the grading system, resulting in an overall net negative outcome due to its collateral impact. According to George Akerlof's "Market for Lemons" model, buyers cannot trust cars they are purchasing if bad ones are indistinguishable from the good, resulting in a loss of market credibility. Similarly, once self-determined grade changes are widely adopted, causing skewed grading distributions, talented students would be indistinguishable from the others, resulting in grades becoming useless. This would cause harm to future employers who are unable to determine student aptitude from academic performance and therefore unknowingly hire unqualified applicants. This would also impact other students deserving of professional opportunities who no longer stand out. On the other hand, this only benefits the individual student who would otherwise have scored lower. Given the small magnitude of the positive happiness compared to the large magnitude of the negative happiness, this would result in an overall net decrease in happiness, making the action morally unjustifiable.

One may argue that as long as students who modify their grades are those who were graded unfairly the first time, this would result in more accurate grading. Fixing the discrepancy corrects the negative impact on other students, and actually benefits the teaching team and employers since the grade will now be more accurate, which results in a positive outcome. Although this sounds plausible, rule utilitarianism states that moral rules must be adopted by everyone. This mirrors Quinn's own analysis of the Nachi worm — a benevolent exploit designed to patch security holes, yet judged wrong because its universal adoption caused more collateral harm than good. Likewise, even well-intentioned grade corrections fail here: if this rule is universalized, all students who feel unfairly graded would exploit the vulnerability. Since there are no objective peer grading audits, there is nothing stopping the vast number of students who overvalue their submissions from making their own grade modifications. Consequently, the corrected grade the counterargument relies on becomes indistinguishable from the inflated ones, so the very accuracy it depends on is exactly what collapses, and under rule utilitarianism the total happiness is decreased by this rule's adoption.

Although a student adjusting their grade may seem harmless, rule utilitarianism shows this results in a net-negative outcome, deeming it morally unjustifiable.
