# Draft

Rule utilitarianism is an ethical framework that evaluates general rules by their net positive or negative impact. Under rule utilitarianism, a student exploiting a security flaw to modify their own grades is morally unjustifiable.

The general rule permitting grade-exploitation would strip the grading system's integrity, resulting in a net negative outcome due to its collateral impact. In George Akerlof's "Market for Lemons," buyers cannot trust cars if the bad ones are indistinguishable from the good, collapsing market credibility. Similarly, once self-made grade changes skew grading distributions, talented students become indistinguishable from the rest, rendering grades useless as a signal of ability. This harms future employers, who can no longer gauge aptitude from grades and unknowingly hire unqualified applicants over stronger candidates. It also harms deserving students who, robbed of a fair signal, no longer stand out among their peers. The only benefit accrues to the individual student who would otherwise have scored lower, a gain far outweighed by the diffuse harm, yielding a net decrease in happiness and making the action morally unjustifiable.

One may argue that if only students who were genuinely graded unfairly modify their grades, the result is more accurate grading. Correcting the discrepancy benefits other students, the teaching team, and employers, since the grade is now more accurate, a positive outcome. Although plausible, rule utilitarianism evaluates a rule by the consequences of its universal adoption. This mirrors Quinn's own analysis of the Nachi worm: a benevolent exploit built to patch security holes, yet judged wrong because its universal adoption caused more collateral harm than good. Likewise, once universalized, this rule lets every student who feels unfairly graded exploit the vulnerability. With no objective peer-grading audits, nothing stops the many students who overvalue their work from altering their own grades. The corrected grades the counterargument relies on become indistinguishable from the inflated ones, so the very accuracy it depends on is exactly what collapses. Worse, even the exploiting student gains nothing: once everyone inflates, their own altered grade is equally worthless, so the sole benefit in the calculus evaporates and total happiness falls under the rule's adoption.

Although adjusting one's own grade may seem harmless to the individual, rule utilitarianism shows that generalizing this action produces a net-negative outcome, making it morally unjustifiable.

# Outline

## ¶1 — Introduction / Thesis
- Define the framework: rule utilitarianism evaluates *general rules* by the net happiness produced if the rule were universally adopted (not the isolated act).
- State the thesis: under rule utilitarianism, a student exploiting a security flaw to modify their own grades is **morally unjustifiable**.

## ¶2 — Main Argument: the rule destroys the grade's value (net negative)
- Name the rule under evaluation: "a student may exploit a security flaw to modify their own grade."
- Core mechanism: universal adoption strips the grading system's integrity → collateral harm.
- **Evidence / analogy — Akerlof's "Market for Lemons":** when good and bad are indistinguishable, buyers stop trusting the whole market; grades work the same way.
- Apply the analogy: skewed distributions → talented students indistinguishable from the rest → grades become useless as a signal of ability.
- Walk the ledger of affected parties (the utilitarian calculus):
  - **Employers** (–): can't gauge aptitude, hire unqualified applicants over stronger ones.
  - **Deserving students** (–): lose their fair signal, no longer stand out.
  - **Exploiting student** (+): the *only* beneficiary; would otherwise have scored lower.
- Weigh it: small concentrated benefit vs. large diffuse harm → net decrease in happiness → morally unjustifiable.

## ¶3 — Counterargument + Rebuttal
- **Counterargument (flip a ledger entry to positive):** if *only* genuinely-mis-graded students exploit, grades become *more* accurate → benefits other students, teaching team, and employers.
- **Rebuttal, step 1 — the RU method:** rule utilitarianism evaluates a rule by the consequences of its *universal* adoption, not the best-case honest subset.
- **Rebuttal, step 2 — course evidence (Quinn's Nachi worm):** a *benevolent* exploit built to patch security holes, still judged wrong because universal adoption caused more collateral harm than good → good intent doesn't survive universalization.
- **Rebuttal, step 3 — no enforceable limit:** with no objective peer-grading audits, the rule can't be restricted to genuine cases; the many students who overvalue their work will also exploit it.
- **Rebuttal, step 4 — the benefit self-destructs:** corrected grades become indistinguishable from inflated ones → the *accuracy* the counterargument depends on is exactly what collapses.
- **Rebuttal, step 5 — even the exploiter loses:** once everyone inflates, the cheater's own grade is worthless too → the sole positive in the calculus evaporates → total happiness falls.

## ¶4 — Conclusion
- Concede the surface intuition: adjusting one's own grade may *seem* harmless to the individual.
- Restate the verdict on RU grounds: generalizing the action produces a net-negative outcome → morally unjustifiable.
