**Context**: To keep operating in China, Apple removes apps its government bans — VPNs, foreign social networks, and world-news apps.

**Setup**: Rule Utilitarianism judges an action by the rule behind it, choosing the rule whose general adoption maximizes net welfare. Because China holds leverage over Apple's supply chain (most iPhones are assembled there), refusing a removal demand realistically means being forced out entirely, not just having those apps blocked. So the workable choice is one of two rules: comply-and-stay vs. refuse-and-exit.

**Thesis**: Under Rule Utilitarianism, it is ethical for Apple to remove these apps to keep operating in China.

**Argument 1 (no benefit — futility)**: Refusing wins nothing for the people it is meant to help. Most banned apps (foreign social networks, world news) are *already* blocked by the Great Firewall regardless of Apple, and for any that aren't, refusing forces Apple out — so users lose those apps either way. The refuse-rule therefore has no offsetting benefit.

**Argument 2 (big cost — collateral harm)**: Refusing inflicts a large *new* harm on a group that has nothing to do with the banned apps. Forced off iPhones, the many millions of ordinary Chinese users would move to domestic phones with documented surveillance and censorship built in — downgrading the security of an enormous population. *(This is the load-bearing argument: it is the only entry that actively tips the calculus toward comply, so it gets the most development.)*

    >**Evidence**: A 2021 Lithuanian government audit found built-in censorship and covert data-harvesting on a leading Chinese phone brand — the kind of domestic device the many millions of Chinese iPhone users would be pushed onto if Apple exited.

**Weighing (explicit tally)**: Refusing produces *no* offsetting benefit (Argument 1) but a large new harm (Argument 2), while complying preserves both those users' security and the wider ecosystem. Comparing the two rules, comply-and-stay is clearly the less harmful, so it maximizes net welfare.

**Counterargument**: By complying, Apple becomes complicit and normalizes censorship, teaching authoritarian governments the tactic works — so the ethical rule is for companies to refuse.

**Rebuttal**: That rule only works if every company refuses together, which fails Quinn's "workable" requirement — a valid rule must be one people actually follow, and industry-wide coordination won't happen. So one company refusing changes nothing except which firm serves the market, and the "normalization" harm is speculative and long-run while the harms of exit are concrete and immediate.

    >**Evidence**: Google's 2010 exit from China shows a principled refusal changes nothing: the censorship carried on and users simply switched to Baidu.

**Conclusion**: Complying feels wrong because it looks like surrender to authoritarian censorship demands. But weighed through the RU lens, refusing helps no one, harms an enormous population, and fails to stop the very censorship it protests — so removing the apps is the rule that produces the least net negative impact, and is therefore the ethical choice.
