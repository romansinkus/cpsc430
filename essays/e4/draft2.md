**Context**: To keep operating in China, Apple removes apps its government bans — VPNs, foreign social networks, and world-news apps.

**Setup**: Rule Utilitarianism judges an action by the general practice behind it, choosing the rule whose widespread adoption maximizes net welfare — so the question is not just what Apple should do once, but which rule tech companies should follow. Because China holds leverage over Apple's supply chain (most iPhones are assembled there), the realistic practices are two: "comply with host-country content restrictions to stay in the market, absent grave and direct rights violations" vs. "refuse such demands and exit." The bounding clause matters — this is not "obey any law," so demands that directly endangered users (e.g., handing over dissidents' locations) would fall outside the rule. (Partial compliance is not a third rule — a banned app is either in the store or not, so it changes no app-level outcome.)

**Thesis**: Under Rule Utilitarianism, it is ethical for Apple to remove these apps to keep operating in China.

**Argument 1 (no benefit — futility)**: As a general practice, refusing wins nothing for the people it is meant to help. Foreign social networks and world-news apps are *already* blocked by the Great Firewall regardless of the store. VPNs are the hardest case, since they exist to bypass the firewall — but China already IP-blocks VPN servers aggressively, and under the refuse-rule the company is forced out anyway, so users lose the maintained VPN client either way. So the refuse-practice delivers no offsetting benefit.

**Argument 2 (big cost — collateral harm)**: The refuse-practice inflicts a large *new* harm on a group with nothing to do with the banned apps. When companies exit rather than comply, the many millions of ordinary users are pushed off secure phones onto domestic devices with documented surveillance and censorship built in — systematically raising the security risk for an enormous population. *(Load-bearing argument: the only entry that actively tips the calculus toward comply, so it gets the most development.)*

    >**Evidence**: A 2021 Lithuanian government audit found built-in censorship and covert data-harvesting on a leading Chinese phone brand — the kind of domestic device the many millions of Chinese iPhone users would be pushed onto if Apple exited.

**Weighing (explicit tally)**: Compared as general practices, refusing produces *no* offsetting benefit (Argument 1) but a large new harm (Argument 2), while complying preserves both those users' security and the wider ecosystem. The comply-rule is therefore the less harmful of the two and maximizes net welfare.

**Counterargument**: By complying, Apple becomes complicit and normalizes censorship, teaching authoritarian governments the tactic works — so the ethical rule is for companies to refuse.

**Rebuttal**: That rule only reduces harm if every company refuses together, which fails Quinn's "workable" requirement — a valid rule must be one people actually follow, and industry-wide coordination won't happen. So the realistic refuse-practice changes nothing except which firm serves the market, and its "normalization" harm is speculative and long-run while the harms of exit are concrete and immediate.

    >**Evidence**: Google's 2010 exit from China shows a principled refusal changes nothing: the censorship carried on and users simply switched to Baidu.

**Conclusion**: Complying feels wrong because it looks like surrender to authoritarian censorship demands. But weighed as a general practice through the RU lens, refusing helps no one, harms an enormous population, and fails to stop the very censorship it protests — so complying is the rule that produces the least net negative impact, and is therefore the ethical choice.
