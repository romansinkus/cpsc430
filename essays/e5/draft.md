Thesis: Under Kantianism, it is unethical for someone to use a service like Napster to download music without paying for it.

Argument 1: When we universalize the rule (first formulation), this results in everyone not paying for music -> the market collapses and artists can no longer sustain their careers, so the maxim contradicts itself.

Evidence: A BBC report on Napster noted music-industry revenue fell to about half its 1999 peak in the years after its launch -> evidence that a maxim of taking music without paying cannot be universalized without eroding the market that produces music.

Argument 2: Downloading treats creators merely as a means (second formulation) — using their work while overriding their "will" that access be paid for, just to save a few dollars.

Counterargument: Downloading doesn't treat the artist merely as a means, because you would never have paid otherwise, so no sale is lost and you're actually expanding their audience and helping their career.

Rebuttal: Kant judges by respect for a person's will, not consequences, so even if no sale is lost and the artist benefits, overriding the terms they set (payment) without consent still treats them merely as a means — consent, not benefit, is the test.

Conclusion: Downloading feels harmless because no one visibly loses out, but the maxim behind it cannot be universalized and uses creators without their consent, so on both formulations it is unethical.
