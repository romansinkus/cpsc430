
Under Kantianism, it is unethical for someone to use a service like Napster to download music without paying.

Universal rule adoption aligns with Kantianism's first formulation: everyone evades payment and a consequential market collapse leaves artists unable to sustain their careers, resulting a contradiction. A BBC report on Napster indicated that in years following the platform's launch, music industry revenue fell to roughly half of its peak in 1999. This pattern exemplifies the reality of universal adoption and its inevitable erosion of the music industry. Universal adoption without constraints placed on Napster would have likely exacerbated this effect leading to much greater financial impacts to the artists indicating real-world impacts of moral rule adoption and when universalized would very likely result in a contradiction.

Downloading treats creators merely as a mean which aligns with Kantianism's 2F: users override the will of artists who desire payment for accessing their content. Artists can opt to create and distribute free music, however the vast majority of artists choose to charge listeners. Professional musicians rely on music sales making it perfectly understandable why payment is not only desired by also necessary. Bypassing payment and using peer-to-peer platforms like Napster directly contradicts their will and treats artists as a means to an end since users are treating musicians as music-producing entities rather than as ends in themselves.

One may argue that downloading music from the P2P platform does not treat artists merely a means to an end since people may have not otherwise paid for the music causing no loss of sales while introducing the upside of growing the audience and therefore helping the artist's long-term career. Despite this possible plausible result of the platform, Kantianism judges ethics by respecting a person's will and not the consequences of one's actions. Therefore, even if an artists objectively benefits from platform utilization, this still overrides the artists' terms: people must pay to access their music. Since using the platform results in non-consensual music access, this practice is unethical since the ethical test hinge on the will of those effected rather than potential positive consequences.

Although using the platform may appear to be harmless at face value, analyzing this decision under Kantianism points towards a clear unethical decision regardless through the lens of both formulations.
