**(1)** Kantianism judges an action by the moral rule behind it: an act is ethical only if that rule can be universalized without contradiction and treats people as ends in themselves, never merely as a means.  
**(2)** Under Kantianism, it is unethical for someone to use a service like Napster to download music without paying.

**(1)** Downloading without paying **fails the first formulation** because the rule behind it **cannot be universalized**.  
**(2)** If everyone took **music without paying** -> **no** one would **sell** music -> the paid-music **market** would **collapse** -> **nothing** left to **download**.  
**(3)** The **rule destroys** the very **practice** it **depends on**, making it impossible to **adopt** as a **universal law** without **contradiction**.  
**(4)** This **mirrors Kant's own example** of the false promise: a rule permitting lying promises cannot be universalized, because if everyone made them no one would believe promises and the practice would disappear.  
**(5)** MUSIC RULE collapses same way.  
**(6)** A BBC report on Napster noted that music-industry revenue fell to roughly half its 1999 peak in the years after the platform launched, illustrating that widespread free-taking really does **erode the market** that produces music, exactly as a **self-defeating rule would predict**.

**(1)** Downloading also treats creators merely as a means, violating the second formulation.  
**(2)** Kantianism does **not forbid** using someone as a means in part; a listener always uses an artist to be entertained.  
**(3)** What it **forbids** is treating a person only as a means, with no regard for their will.  
**(4)** Artists set the terms for their work, and the vast majority choose to charge listeners.  
**(5)** By bypassing payment on a peer-to-peer platform like Napster, users override the artist's will and treat them as nothing more than a music-producing resource rather than as an autonomous, rational being whose choices deserve respect.  
**(6)** In this sense, downloading for free is no different from taking groceries from a shop without paying: the seller set a term, payment, and the taker simply ignored it.

**(1)** One may argue that downloading does not treat artists merely as a means, since someone who would never have paid causes no lost sale and may even grow the artist's audience, helping their long-term career.  
**(2)** However, Kantianism judges an action by respect for a person's will, not by its consequences.  
**(3)** Even if the artist objectively benefits, downloading still overrides the terms they set — that access be paid for — without their consent.  
**(4)** Because the access is non-consensual, the benefit is beside the point: consent, not outcome, is the test, so the practice remains unethical.  

**(1)** Although using the platform seems harmless at face value, examining it under Kantianism yields a clear verdict.
**(2)** It fails the first formulation as a self-defeating rule, and the second by using creators without their consent, and is therefore unethical.
