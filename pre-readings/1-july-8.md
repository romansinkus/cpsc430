### 1: Developing Strong Thesis Statements

* **Thesis or main claim MUST be debatable**

  * This means that people should be able to have different opinions about what you are arguing
  * If you are arguing something that is a fact, then there's no reason anyone would try to refute it
  * Example of non-debatable claim: "pollution is bad for the environment"
  * Example of debatable claim: "at least 25% of the federal budget should be spent on limiting pollution"
  * Example of debatable claim: "america should focus its anti-pollution efforts of privately owned cars"
* **Thesis must be narrow**

  * Example of thesis that it too broad: "drug use is detrimental to society"
    * There are too many questions the claim leaves open
  * Example of a narrow/focused thesis: "Illegal drug use is detrimental because it encourages gang violence"
  * Qualifiers like "*typically*", "*generally*", "*usually*", and "*on average*" help limit the scope of the claim
* **Types of claims**

  * Claims of fact or definition
    * argues the definition of something
  * Claims of cause and effect
    * X caused Y
  * Claims about value
    * X is the most Y right now
  * Claims about solutions or policies
    * We should do X instead of Y

### 2: Writing assignments

* The general structure should be open -> narrow -> open
* Introduction:
  * The thesis generally comes at the end of the end of the intro
  * The thesis is the most specific part of the introduction
* Body paragraphs:
  * Topic sentence

  * Support
    * Evidence + explanation
  * Unity

* Conclusion:
  * Brief summary of the main points AND/OR restatement of the thesis

### 6: Logic in Argumentative Writing

**a. Logic in Argumentative Writing**

* Non-logical arguments (statements that cannot be proven or disproven) are often often important in argumentative writing
  * This involves appealing to emotion
* Illogical arguments are ones that are false -> these should be avoided
* To be logical, a proposition must be tested within a logical sequence
* Syllogism is the most famous logical sequence

**b. Using Logic**

* Premise, conclusion, argument, syllogism, enthymeme, induction, deduction
* Syllogistic Fallacies

**c. Logical Fallacies**

* *Fallacies* are common errors in reasoning
* *Slippery Slope*: implying that B, C, etc. will happen if A happens
* *Hasty Generalization*: conclusion based on insufficient or biased evidence -> rushing to a conclusion before you have all the facts
* *Post hoc ergo propter hoc*: If B happens after A, then A must have caused B
* *Genetic Fallacy*: the origins of something determine its character
* *Begging the Claim*: The conclusion that the writer should prove is validated within the claim
* *Circular Argument*: restate argument instead of actually proving it.
  * e.g. George Bush is a good communicator because he speaks effectively
* either/or: oversimplifies argument by reducing it to only two sides or choices
  * e.g. We can either stop using cars or destroy the earth
* Ad hominem: attack on a person rather than their argument
  * e.g. Green Peace's strategies aren't effective because they are all dirty, lazy hippies
* Ad populum/Bandwagon Appeal: presents what most people think in order to convince you
  * e.g. If you were a true American you would support the rights of people to choose whatever vehicle they want
* Red Herring: Avoid key arguments rather than addressing them
  * e.g. The level of mercury in seafood may be unsafe, but what will fishers do to support their families?
* Straw Man: oversimplifies someone's viewpoint then attacks the simplified view
  * e.g. People who don't support the proposed state minimum wage increase hate the poor.
* Moral Equivalences: comparing minor misdeeds with major atrocities suggesting that both are equally immoral
  * e.g. That parking attendant who gave me a ticket is as bad as Hitler

**d. Using Logic in Writing**

* When converting logical syllogisms into written arguments, remeber to...
  * lay out each premise clearly
  * provide evidence for each premise
  * draw a clear connection to the conclusion

**e. Does Logic Always Work?**

* Arguments must often spend as much time convincing audiences of the legitimacy of the premises as the legitimacy of the conclusions
