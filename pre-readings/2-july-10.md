## 2.1 Introduction, Defining Terms, Rationalizations, Four Scenarios, Overview of Ethical Theories

* Community advantages outweight the disadvantages
* People share the core values of life, happiness, and the ability to achieve goals
* **Morality**: what society dictates you should or shouldn't do in certain situations
* People can be in multiple societies which leads to moral dilemmas (e.g. pacific by *religion* needing to serve in the army by *law*)
* **Ethics:** rational behaviour of a person's moral beliefs or behaviour
* Supposedly almost half of us think we are better than everyone else we know
* **Rationalization**: moral excuse, a justification for an immoral action

  * Way of "short circuiting" an ethical analysis of a situation
  * We find a reason to explain why it's alright to do something
  * Examples:
    * Everyone else is doing it, it's normal. Who am I to say it's wrong?
    * I am taking care of my family, etc.
    * This isn't my call. My boss ordered me to do it.
    * Trying to achieve an important goal and cannot let anything get in the way of successfully reaching it.
    * *See textbook for more examples*
* When two people reach different conclusions, we can weigh the **facts** and the **reasoning** process behind their conclusions to determine the stronger line of thinking
* Ethics is focused on voluntary, moral choices rather than involuntary ones or ones that are not in the moral realm (e.g. picking the color of a car is voluntary but not a moral decision)

  * Reflex actions do not qualify as a reasoned choice
* A good ethical theory should enable you to make a persuasive, logical argument to a diverse audience
* We reject proposed ethical theories that are not based on reasoning from facts or commonly accepted valuesA2.2: Subjective Relativism
* **Ethical relativism**: the theory that there are no universal moral norms of right and wrong

  * People can have opposite views of a moral problem and both be right
  * There is **subjective relativism** and **cultural relativism**
* **Subjective Relativism**: each person decides right and wrong for himself/herself

  * "What's right for you may not be right for me"
* Subjective relativism provides an easy excuse for someone whose conduct is being questioned -> they can just say "who are you to tell me what I should and should not do?"
* Modification of subjective relativism: "I can decide what's rigt for me, as long as my actions don't hurt anybody else" but this is actually inconsistent with subjective relativism
* Tolerance != subjective relativism
* We reject subjective relativism as a workable ethical theory

  * According to SR, nobody's conclusions are more valid that others'

### 2.3: Cultural Relativism

* Cultural relativism is teh ethical theory that the meaninng of right and wrong rests with a society's actual moral guidlines. These guidelines vary from place to place and from time to time.
* Different social contexts demand different moral guidlines
* Cultural relativism provides no way out -> no way for the two sides to find common ground
* **Many/any fallacy:** judging many options to be acceptable and then reaching the conclusion that any option is acceptable
  * E.g. there are many ways to comment code that are good but this doesn't mean that any way to commend code is good
* There are some core values between shared between cultures.
  * e.g. caring for infants, prohibiting murder, etc.
* Cultural relativism is not a powerful tool for constructing ethical evaluations persuasive to a diverse audience therefore we cannot use it

### 2.4: Divine Command Theory

* **Divine command theory** is based on the idea that good actions are those aligned with the will of God and bad actions are those contrary to the will of God
  * Since the holy books contain God's directions, we can use the holy books as moral decision-making guides
* Holy books are not updated so they don't include topics like the internet

### 2.5 Ethical Egoism

* The morally right action for a person to take in a particular situation is the action that will provide that perwson with the maximum long-term benefit
* This ideology doesn't meant that you cannot help others, but that when you help others, you should do it with your long-term best interest
* It's better to let other people take care of themselves
* Dependence on the charity of others leads to a loss of self-esteem
* Focusing on yourself may indirectly help other people
  * e.g. Sucessful entrepeneurs may make a lot of money for themselves, but they also create jobs that strengthen the economy
* It's in a person's best long-term self-interest to NOT break promises
  * Same idea for lying and cheating

### 2.6: Kantianism

* Morality through reasoning. You can explain exactly why things are right and wrong
* What we want to do is of no importance. We should focus on instead what we ought to do.
* Categorical Imperative (First Formulation): Act only from moral rules that you can at the same time will to be universal moral laws
* Moral rules can self-distruct when we try to make it a universal law
  * e.g. someone making a promise with the intent of breaking it
* Categorical Imperative (Second Formulation): Act so that you always treat both yourself and other people as ends in themselves, and never only as a means to an end.
  * i.e. wrong for one person to "use" another
  * e.g. semi-conductor factory worker example
* **Kantianism treats all persons as moral equals**
* Aligns with the view that manipulating others is wrong
* If you have a problem between a perfect and an imperfect duty, the perfect duty must prevail
