### Detailed Summary

Chapter 2, Secs. 2.7–2.12 (Quinn, *Ethics for the Information Age*). Continues the survey of ethical theories, focusing on the ones Quinn considers **workable**: act utilitarianism, rule utilitarianism, social contract theory, and virtue ethics (joining Kantianism from 2.6). Each theory gets a definition, a worked scenario, a "case for," and a "case against."

### 2.7 Act Utilitarianism

* Proposed by **Jeremy Bentham (1748–1832)** and **John Stuart Mill (1806–1873)** — sharp contrast to Kantianism.
* Core idea: an action is good if its **benefits exceed its harms**, bad if its harms exceed its benefits. Based on the **principle of utility** (a.k.a. the **Greatest Happiness Principle**).
* **Utility**: the tendency of an object to produce happiness or prevent unhappiness for an individual or a community. Depending on context, "happiness" = advantage/benefit/good/pleasure, and "unhappiness" = disadvantage/cost/evil/pain.

  > "An action is right (or wrong) to the extent that it increases (or decreases) the total happiness of the affected parties." — Principle of Utility
  >
* **Consequentialist** theory — only the *consequences* of an action matter, not the motive behind it. Bentham:

  > "There is no such thing as any sort of motive that is in itself a bad one. If [motives] are good or bad, it is only on account of their effects."
  >
* **Act utilitarianism** specifically: an action is good if its net effect (summed over *all affected beings*) produces more happiness than unhappiness. Method: assign pleasure a positive number, pain a negative number, add up the change in happiness across everyone. Positive sum → good; negative sum → bad. The moral action is the one producing the **maximum increase** (or, if all options are negative, the **minimum decrease**).
* Note the word **"beings," not "persons"** — an act utilitarian must decide which beings are **morally significant**. Bentham: any being that can experience pain and pleasure counts. That includes women and people of color (historically excluded), and also **all mammals (and perhaps other animals)**. This means **environmental impacts** often must be included in the calculus.

**2.7.2 Highway scenario (worked example)**

* A state considers replacing a curvy stretch of highway with a straighter one. Everything is translated into dollars ("benefit"/"cost" instead of "happiness"/"unhappiness").
* Costs: $20M to compensate ~150 condemned homes + $10M construction + $1M lost animal habitat = **$31M cost**.
* Benefits: 15,000 cars/weekday save 1 mile each; at 40¢/mile that's $6,000/weekday, over a 25-year lifetime = **$39M benefit**.
* Since benefit ($39M) > cost ($31M), building the highway is a **good action**.
* **Commentary / caution**: reducing everything to dollars is controversial (e.g., how do you price a homeowner's emotional distress at being forced out?), but putting everything in common units is the only way to add "apples and oranges."

**Bentham's seven attributes** for weighting a given pleasure or pain (not all benefits are equal):

* **Intensity** — magnitude of the experience
* **Duration** — how long it lasts
* **Certainty** — probability it will actually happen
* **Propinquity** — how close it is in space and time
* **Fecundity** — its ability to produce more experiences of the same kind
* **Purity** — the extent pleasure isn't diluted by pain (or vice versa)
* **Extent** — number of people affected

**2.7.3 The case FOR act utilitarianism**

1. **It focuses on happiness** — fits the intuition that the purpose of life is to be happy.
2. **It is practical** — gives a straightforward procedure; done openly with all info shared, it's a good way for a diverse group to reach a collective decision (e.g., a citizen panel siting an unwanted prison via public hearings and published scores).
3. **It is comprehensive** — lets the agent weigh all elements of a situation (e.g., the harm of telling your mother the truth about her awful haircut can tilt the balance toward a white lie).

**2.7.4 The case AGAINST act utilitarianism**

1. **Unclear where to draw the line** — you must decide whom to include and how far into the future to look, and that choice changes the outcome (the highway example ignored severed neighborhoods, changed commutes, effects past 25 years, etc.). "We must draw the line somewhere."
2. **Not practical to do for every decision** — the full calculus takes too much time/effort. *Response*: act utilitarians can use moral **"rules of thumb"** (e.g., "It is wrong to lie") but reserve the right to override them after detailed analysis when circumstances warrant.
3. **Ignores our innate sense of duty** — gives no weight to obligation. W. D. Ross's example: if keeping a promise to A produces 1,000 units of good but breaking it produces 1,001 units for B, act utilitarianism says break the promise — yet most people say keep your word. (You can't escape by adding "hurt feelings" of −N; just re-scale B's benefit to 1,001 + N.) The real issue: "doing the right thing" has a value that's hard to quantify.
4. **Cannot predict consequences with certainty** — we may misjudge certainty/intensity/duration or forget consequences, leading to the wrong choice.
5. **Problem of moral luck** — it's questionable whether an action's moral worth should depend *solely* on consequences that aren't fully in the agent's control. Example: sending flowers to a hospitalized aunt who then suffers a violent allergic reaction — act utilitarianism labels the gift "bad," which "doesn't seem fair."

* Verdict: imperfect, but an objective, rational theory that lets you explain *why* an action is right or wrong. **Workable**, alongside Kantianism.

### 2.8 Rule Utilitarianism

* Developed to fix act utilitarianism's weaknesses. (Some argue Mill was actually a rule utilitarian.)
* **Rule utilitarianism**: we ought to adopt those **moral rules** that, *if followed by everyone*, lead to the greatest increase in total happiness over all affected parties.
* Key contrast: a rule utilitarian applies the principle of utility to **moral rules**; an act utilitarian applies it to **individual actions**.
* Both rule utilitarianism and Kantianism are rule-focused and may derive overlapping rules, **but for different reasoIt's ns**: a rule utilitarian follows a rule because its *universal adoption maximizes net happiness* (looking at consequences); a Kantian follows a rule because it accords with the *Categorical Imperative* (looking at the will/motive).

**2.8.2 Nachi worm scenario (worked example)**

* Background: In Aug 2003 the **Blaster** worm exploited a Windows security hole and made infected PCs reboot repeatedly. A second worm, **Nachi**, exploited the *same* hole but was benevolent — it deleted Blaster, auto-downloaded Microsoft's patch, then used the PC to seek out other vulnerable machines.
* Question: Was releasing Nachi morally right or wrong?
* Method: propose a moral rule and test whether its **universal adoption** increases happiness. Proposed rule: *"If I can write and release a helpful worm that improves the security of the computers it infects, I should do so."*
* **Benefits** if universally adopted: people who don't patch would benefit from automatic fixes.
* **Harms** if universally adopted:
  * Every new harmful worm would trigger many counter-worms → tons of extra network traffic (Nachi actually disabled Diebold ATM networks at two banks).
  * Even benevolent worms may contain **bugs** that damage data/programs.
  * Extra burden on **system administrators**, who must fight *every* new worm since they can't immediately tell if it's harmful.
* Conclusion: harms outweigh benefits → releasing the Nachi worm is **morally wrong**.

**2.8.3 The case FOR rule utilitarianism**

1. **Not every decision requires the full calculus** — relying on rules saves time/effort.
2. **Exceptional situations don't overthrow moral rules** — solves the Ross promise dilemma: everyone keeping promises produces more long-term good than giving everyone liberty to break them, so keep the promise to A.
3. **Solves the problem of moral luck** — it cares about the *typical* result, so a freak outcome (the allergic-reaction flowers) doesn't make the action bad. Sending flowers to hospital patients is a good action.
4. **Reduces the problem of bias** — act utilitarianism tempts you to ask "Is it okay for *me* to do this?" and inflate personal benefits. Rule utilitarianism asks "Is it okay for *everyone* in a similar circumstance to do this?" — likelier to weight benefits/harms fairly.
5. **Appeals to a wide cross-section of society** — Bernard Gert notes utilitarianism is "paradoxically, the kind of moral theory usually held by people who claim that they have no moral theory" (e.g., "It is all right to do anything as long as no one gets hurt"). What matters is whether allowing the action *as a rule* brings greater net happiness than forbidding it.

**2.8.4 The case AGAINST utilitarianism IN GENERAL** (applies to *both* act and rule)

1. **Forces a single scale for completely different consequences** — all consequences must be put in the same units to be added. Some are easy (dollars), but intangibles are hard: e.g., a sociologist predicts condemning 150 homes causes 5 divorces — how do you price that? In some cases utilitarians must **quantify the value of a human life**.
2. **Ignores an unjust distribution of good** — the calculus only cares about *total* happiness. Example: option A gives everyone 100 units; option B gives half the people 201 each and the other half nothing. Utility says B is superior (higher total), which strikes many as wrong. A "greatest good for the greatest number" fix actually smuggles in a *second* principle — **distributive justice** — which isn't pure utilitarianism and can conflict with the principle of utility.

* Verdict: rule utilitarianism treats all persons as equals and lets adherents give reasons — a **third workable theory** (with Kantianism and act utilitarianism).

### 2.9 Social Contract Theory

* Motivating case: after the 2003 Iraq invasion, when Baghdad police vanished, residents looted ministries and bought AK-47s for self-protection — is this the typical response to a lack of governmental authority?
* **Thomas Hobbes (1588–1679)**, *Leviathan*: without rules and enforcement, no one builds anything of value (they can't be sure of keeping it). Life in the **"state of nature"** would be lived in "continuall feare, and danger of violent death," and would be "solitary, poore, nasty, brutish, and short."

  * Rational people see cooperation is essential, but it requires mutually agreed guidelines. Moral rules are "simply the rules that are necessary if we are to gain the benefits of social living."
  * Everyone in a civilized society has implicitly agreed to (1) a set of moral rules governing relations among citizens and (2) a government to enforce them — the **social contract**.
* **Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778)**, *The Social Contract*: "all legitimate authority among men must be based on covenants." Everyone gives themselves and their rights to the whole community; since no one is above the rules, no one wants to impose unfair burdens on others (that would burden themselves).
* Enforcement is essential — it's easy to rationalize selfish behavior (e.g., Bill cheating $2,000 on taxes because it's "a drop in the bucket" to the government). What restrains him is knowing he'll be **punished** if caught. Society needs both laws *and* a system to enforce them.
* **James Rachels'** definition:

  > "Morality consists in the set of rules, governing how people are to treat one another, that rational people will agree to accept, for their mutual benefit, on the condition that others follow those rules as well."
  >

**Rights and duties**

* Hobbes, **Locke**, and others held all morally significant beings have certain rights (life, liberty, property; some moderns add **privacy**).
* Rights ↔ duties: your right to life imposes on others the duty not to kill you.
  * **Negative right**: guaranteed by others *leaving you alone* (e.g., free expression).
  * **Positive right**: obligates others to *do something* for you (e.g., a free education).
  * **Absolute right**: guaranteed without exception (negative rights like the right to life usually count).
  * **Limited right**: may be restricted by circumstances (positive rights usually count — e.g., states fund K–12 but not all of higher education).
* Social contract theorists evaluate problems from the viewpoint of **rights**; Kantians from **duties** (Kant argued rights follow from duties).

**2.9.2 Rawls's Theory of Justice**

* **John Rawls (1921–2002)** revived social contract theory. Society mixes cooperation (produces more benefits than going solo) and competition (over how benefits are divided). A well-ordered society must set rights/duties and justly distribute "the benefits and burdens of social cooperation."
* **Veil of ignorance**: principles of justice are chosen from an "original position" where each person is ignorant of their future sex, race, ethnicity, wealth, intelligence, abilities, etc. Agreements reached this way are fair — no one picks principles that could harm them if they turn out disadvantaged.
* **Rawls's two principles of justice**:

  1. Each person may claim a "fully adequate" number of **basic rights and liberties** (thought, speech, association, safety, property), so long as these are consistent with everyone else having the same.
  2. Social/economic **inequalities** are acceptable only if: (a) they attach to positions everyone has a **fair and equal opportunity** to attain, and (b) they are "to the greatest benefit of the least-advantaged members of society (the **difference principle**)."
* First principle explained: equal opportunity means two people of equal intelligence/talent/motivation should have equal odds of reaching an advantaged position, regardless of birth class (being named Bush or Clinton shouldn't raise your odds of becoming president).
* **Difference principle** explained: an inequality is justified only if its overall effect most benefits the least advantaged.

  * Example (allowed): a **graduated income tax** — Plan B (progressive) is unequal but justified because it benefits the most disadvantaged, vs. Plan A (flat tax).
  * Example (violation): a military draft where poor people are more likely to be drafted than the wealthy.

**2.9.3 Hao scenario (worked example)**

* Hao, a convenience-store chain owner, uses purchase data to build customer profiles (who has an infant, a pet, a barbecue) and **sells them** to mail-order companies. Some customers welcome the catalogs; others resent the junk mail.
* Analysis via rights: the morality turns on whether Hao violated customers' **privacy rights**. If the customer and Hao have *equal* rights to the transaction info, selling it is fine; if customers have a right to expect transactions to be **confidential**, Hao was wrong to sell it without permission.

**2.9.4 The case FOR social contract theory**

1. **Framed in the language of rights** — powerful and attractive in individualistic (Western-style democratic) cultures.
2. **Based on a solid understanding of human nature** — recognizes rational people act out of self-interest absent a common agreement.
   * **Tragedy of the commons**: individuals benefit from exploiting a shared resource while the costs are borne by the whole community. Medieval commons + overgrazing sheep → each shepherd's rational move is to become a **free rider**, but too many free riders destroy the resource. Fix: a law against overgrazing + punishment + enforcement.
   * The common good is best realized when everyone cooperates; cooperation occurs when selfish actors suffer negative consequences. This also answers "if everyone has a right to liberty, how can we imprison criminals?" — punishment of rule-breakers is what restrains people.
3. **Explains when civil disobedience can be morally right** — the 1960 Greensboro Woolworth's lunch-counter sit-ins broke segregation laws, but were morally justified because those laws gave Black people greater burdens and fewer benefits than white people, and so were **unjust**.

**2.9.5 The case AGAINST social contract theory**

1. **None of us signed it** — it's not a real contract. *Response* (Rawls): the contract is **hypothetical and nonhistorical** — it's what reasonable people "could, or would, agree to, not what they have agreed to." The guidelines come from analysis (facts + values + reasoning), not history. It is *not* cultural relativism in disguise.
2. **Some actions can be characterized in multiple ways** (shared with Kantianism) — how you describe a situation affects which rules/rights apply.
3. **Doesn't resolve conflicting rights** (shared with Kantianism) — e.g., abortion pits the mother's right to liberty against the fetus's right to life; debates typically "solve" it by denying the other side's right.
4. **May be unjust to those incapable of upholding their side** — what about people who, through no fault of their own, can't follow the rules? *Response*: distinguish those who *deliberately* break rules (punish) from those who *can't understand* them (care for) — but drawing that line is hard (e.g., treating drug users who steal as criminals vs. as patients).

* Verdict: logical and analytical, treats all persons as equals, lets adherents justify conclusions — a **workable theory**.

### 2.10 Virtue Ethics

* Criticism of Kantianism/utilitarianism/social contract: they ignore moral education, moral wisdom, family/social relationships, and the role of **emotions**. **Virtue ethics** accounts for all of these.
* Unlike the Enlightenment-era theories, virtue ethics traces back to ancient China and Greece. **Confucianism** (5th c. BC) stresses cultivating virtues and strong character. In Greece, **arete** ("virtue"/excellence) = reaching one's highest potential. **Aristotle's** *Nicomachean Ethics* (4th c. BC): true happiness and flourishing come from living a life of virtue.

**2.10.1 Virtues and vices**

* Two kinds of virtue (Aristotle):

  * **Intellectual virtues** — associated with reasoning and truth.
  * **Moral virtues** (a.k.a. virtues of character) — habits/dispositions formed by **repetition** of the relevant virtuous actions (e.g., develop honesty by habitually telling the truth). *This section focuses on moral virtues.*
* A moral virtue is a **deep-seated character trait**: an honest person tells the truth as a matter of course, is uncomfortable even considering deceit, and won't join dishonest activity. Doing right becomes **second nature**.
* A virtue is also a disposition to **feel** a certain way, not just to act. Aristotle:

  > "We may even go so far as to state that the man who does not enjoy performing noble actions is not a good man at all. Nobody would call a man just who does not enjoy acting justly, nor generous who does not enjoy generous actions, and so on."
  >

  * Courage, for instance, is tightly connected to emotion — you must be able to *moderate your fear*.
* Character traits take time to become deep-seated (the **Boy Scout** who starts doing good turns for his scoutmaster's praise but eventually becomes genuinely **benevolent** — helping is now second nature).
* When a virtuous person doesn't exercise the virtue, we assume a good explanation (reliable **Angel** misses a meeting → "Something must have happened").

  > **Summary of Virtue Ethics**: "A right action is an action that a virtuous person, acting in character, would do in the same circumstances. A virtuous person is a person who possesses and lives out the virtues. The virtues are those character traits human beings need in order to flourish and be truly happy."
  >
* Which virtues matter depends partly on culture (Homeric Greece prized physical courage; the American West prized self-reliance; today tolerance) — but core virtues like **honesty, justice, and loyalty** seem universal.
* A **vice** is a character trait that prevents flourishing — the opposite of a virtue. Aristotle's insight: a virtue often sits as a **mean between two vices**, one of excess and one of deficiency:

  * **Courage** = mean between **cowardice** (excess of fear) and **rashness** (deficiency of fear).
  * **Friendliness** = mean between **quarrelsomeness** (too critical) and **obsequiousness** (too uncritical / gives in too easily).
* Virtue ethics attends to the **agent** (the person acting) as well as the act (like Kantianism/social contract) and consequences (like utilitarianism). A good person does "the right thing at the right time for the right reason."
* Moral decision-making can't be reduced to routine rule-application, though "rules of thumb" have a place (e.g., "Keep confidences") — but **moral wisdom/discernment takes precedence over any rule**.

**2.10.2 Josh & Reggie scenario (worked example)**

* Josh (CS senior) is close friends with Reggie, who has given him free rides home many times (Josh never chipped in for gas). On the capstone team, Reggie — normally hardworking/trustworthy — falls apart after his father dies suddenly in a car accident: he drinks too much, skips meetings, and his code fails spec. The team quietly **rewrites his portion**; Reggie presents "his" code without admitting it. Everyone must email the professor grading teammates; poor reviews from all teammates could force Reggie to repeat the class, and he pleads with Josh for a good review because he can't afford more college. What should Josh do?
* Decision: Josh is **honest** (can't bring himself to lie that Reggie did well) but also **just** (feels indebted for the rides) and **compassionate** (Reggie lost his father and couldn't say goodbye). Reflecting, Josh realizes he landed here partly through his *own* past failures — taking the free rides selfishly (had he paid for gas he wouldn't feel so obligated) and not being a good enough friend to talk to Reggie earlier; the team should have told the professor when Reggie first stopped participating.
* Resolution: Josh will be **truthful** with the professor — but not simply report Reggie as mediocre. He'll also **take responsibility** by giving a full account of how his *own* earlier inaction contributed to the outcome.

**2.10.3 The case FOR virtue ethics**

1. **Sometimes it makes more sense to focus on virtues than on obligations/rights/consequences** — e.g., why is stealing to satisfy a selfish desire wrong? Kantianism and rule utilitarianism give complex answers; virtue ethics is simpler: it disrupts your acquisition of the virtue of **honesty**.
2. **Personal relationships can be morally relevant** — the other theories demand complete impartiality, which yields hard-to-accept conclusions. Example: $10,000 to take your own kids to Disneyland vs. feeding 1,000 hungry refugees — utility says feed the refugees, but virtue ethics recognizes that **parents are supposed to be partial toward their own children**.
3. **Recognizes moral decision-making skills develop over time** — virtues and moral wisdom take time; when unsure, consult a **role model** or respected elder who more fully possesses the virtues you seek ("the journey from the-person-I-am to the-person-I-am-meant-to-be").
4. **Recognizes the important role of emotions** — humans aren't "dispassionate calculating machines"; virtuous people feel satisfied doing good and are deeply affected by hard decisions.

**2.10.4 The case AGAINST virtue ethics**

1. **People conceive of human flourishing differently** — in a heterogeneous society there's no agreement on which traits are virtues, so no agreement on what a virtuous person would do. This makes it less powerful than theories claiming **universally true** norms.
2. **Can't guide government policy** — it focuses on the individual agent, but policy is set by agencies/groups. In the highway case (2.7.2) an act utilitarian can compute costs/benefits; virtue ethics can only say officials should be honest/diligent/prudent — nothing more for the analysis.
3. **Undermines holding people responsible for bad actions** — virtues are acquired over time and depend heavily on upbringing, education, and community (all outside a child's control), so how can we hold someone responsible for acquiring vices?

* Verdict: imperfect, but provides a framework to analyze situations, reach conclusions, and justify them logically — a **workable theory**.

### 2.11 Comparing Workable Ethical Theories

* All the surveyed theories (divine command, ethical egoism, Kantianism, act util, rule util, social contract, virtue ethics) share **ethical objectivism**: morality has an existence **outside the human mind** (it is *discovered*, not invented).
* What separates the workable ones from divine command theory: ethical decision-making is a **rational process** using logical reasoning from facts and commonly held values. What separates them from ethical egoism: Kantianism, utilitarianism, social contract, and virtue ethics **explicitly take other people into consideration**.
* Quinn's **five most workable theories**: **Kantianism, act utilitarianism, rule utilitarianism, social contract theory, virtue ethics.**
* How each answers *"What makes an action morally right?"* (Figure 2.9):
  * **Act utilitarianism** — it results in the maximum net increase in the total good of the affected parties.
  * **Virtue ethics** — it is consistent with the actions of a virtuous person.
  * The rule-based theories — it accords with a correct moral rule. Then *"What makes a moral rule correct?"*:
    * **Kantianism** — everyone can follow it all the time without a logical contradiction that undermines the rule (Categorical Imperative).
    * **Rule utilitarianism** — everyone following it all the time yields the greatest increase in total good.
    * **Social contract theory** — rational people would collectively accept it as binding for the resulting benefits to the community.
* Summary of focus: act utilitarianism → **consequences**; Kantianism/rule util/social contract → **rules**; virtue ethics → the **agent**.

### 2.12 Morality of Breaking the Law

* **Legal ≠ moral.** An action can be wrong without being illegal (e.g., causing an accident by texting where no anti-texting law exists). The reverse question: can an action be *right* even if it's *illegal*?
* Earlier (2.9) civil disobedience was justified because the *laws* (segregation) were unjust. Here Quinn assumes the law is **just**, then tests one illegal act — **copying a copyrighted CD and giving it to a friend** — against four theories.

**2.12.1 Social contract theory perspective**

* Everyone bears burdens to receive benefits; the legal system protects rights and stops people choosing selfishness over the common good. So we have a **prima facie obligation to obey the law** — meaning, all else equal, be law-abiding; in return your own legal rights are respected. Break it only for a **higher-order moral obligation**.
* Copying the CD violates the copyright owner's legal rights; wanting to do something nice for a friend isn't an overriding concern → **wrong**.

**2.12.2 Kantian perspective**

* Similar to social contract. Property rights rest on an implicit common agreement: declaring an object "yours" means everyone else is obliged to refrain from using it, and justice demands you respect everyone else's similar claims. Copying copyrighted material violates the owner's property rights → failing your obligations to others → **wrong**.

**2.12.3 Rule-utilitarian perspective**

* Laws have value through their **utility** (everyone driving on the same side of the road; taxes funding social goods). If people ignored laws at will: momentary happiness for the lawbreakers, but many harms — direct victims harmed, less respect for law → more lawbreaking → heavier burden on the criminal-justice system → less money for schools/parks/museums. So there are strong general reasons to obey.
* But not simply "Obey the law" — some laws are harmful (e.g., segregation). A more nuanced rule: **"Obey the law unless (1) the law does not maximize utility, and (2) you have good reason to believe the long-term net effects of breaking the law will be greater utility."**
* Does copyright law maximize utility? It's a compromise between creators' rights and public access — the public benefits from the creative works, creators benefit by collecting money for a time. If everyone copied CDs, only consumers would benefit; musicians lose income, publishers stop producing → quantity and quality of music could drop. Long-term effects of breaking the law aren't clearly greater utility → **wrong**.

**2.12.4 Act-utilitarian perspective**

* Unlike the others, act utilitarianism can find a *specific* case where copying is right. Example: you own a great CD; a friend is bedridden for a month after a car accident, has no money for music, and people are fundraising for their medical bills. You copy the CD for them.
* Rough calculus: benefit to friend ≥ $15 (CD price); your happiness at pleasing them = $10; no lost sale (friend wouldn't have bought it, and may even boost sales by recommending it) → record label = $0; unlikely to be prosecuted → legal system = $0. **Total ≈ $25 benefit** vs. $0 for doing nothing → copying is the **right thing** *in this case*.
* Caution: don't extrapolate — this doesn't mean an act-utilitarian analysis *always* finds CD copying acceptable.

**2.12.5 Conclusion**

* There's nothing *intrinsically* immoral about copying a CD, but society has enacted intellectual-property laws. From social contract and Kantianism we have a **prima facie duty to obey the law and respect property rights**; from rule utilitarianism the benefits of "Obey the law" greatly exceed the harms. Across these theories, obey the law unless there's a **strong overriding moral obligation** — and copying a disc to save a few dollars or help a friend doesn't qualify.

### Chapter Summary (key takeaways)

* We live in communities for mutual benefit; **morality** = a society's guidelines for behavior; **ethics** = the rational examination of moral beliefs/behaviors.
* **Ethical relativism** = morality is *invented* (no universal principles); includes **subjective relativism** (individual invents it) and **cultural relativism** (each society invents it). If morality is invented, no set of guidelines is better than another, making ethics nearly impossible — "You can't do ethics without values."
* **Ethical objectivism** = morality exists outside the mind and must be *discovered*; there are universal principles true for all people. All the workable theories rest on this.
* Each of the five practical theories holds a valuable insight:
  * **Kant** — every human is equally valuable; respect each person's rationality and autonomy.
  * **Utilitarians** — consider the consequences of an action.
  * **Social contract theory** — the individual and collective benefit of protecting rights (life, liberty, property).
  * **Virtue ethics** — you can count on a good person to do the right thing, at the right time, in the right way.
* None is perfect. In practice, consider virtues *and* duties *and* rights *and* consequences. When all perspectives agree, decide with confidence; the hard cases (most of the rest of the book) are where no course of action perfectly respects everyone's rights *and* maximizes total happiness — "That's when things get interesting!"

### Concise Summary

The chapter covers four **workable** ethical theories (joining Kantianism from 2.6). All are **objectivist** — morality is *discovered* through reasoning from facts and shared values, and all take other people into account.

#### Act Utilitarianism

* **What it is:** An action is good if its net effect — total happiness minus total unhappiness, summed over *all affected beings* — is positive. Only consequences matter, not motives (consequentialist).
* **Simplified:** Add up the good and bad this *specific action* causes for everyone. More good than bad = right.
* **Example:** Building a straighter highway costs $31M but saves drivers $39M over 25 years → net positive → building it is the right action.
* **Strengths:**
  * Focuses on happiness, matching the intuition that the point of life is to be happy.
  * Practical — gives a clear, step-by-step procedure.
  * Comprehensive — weighs every element of a situation.
* **Weaknesses:**
  * Unclear where to draw the line (whom to count, how far into the future).
  * Impractical to fully calculate for every decision.
  * Ignores our sense of duty (would break a promise for just +1 unit of good).
  * We can't predict consequences with certainty.
  * Vulnerable to the *problem of moral luck* (a well-meant act with a freak bad outcome is judged "bad").

#### Rule Utilitarianism

* **What it is:** Adopt the moral *rules* that would produce the greatest total happiness *if everyone followed them*, then act by those rules. Applies utility to rules, not individual acts.
* **Simplified:** Don't ask "does this act help?" — ask "what if *everyone* did this?" Follow the rule that makes the best world.
* **Example:** Should you release a "helpful" worm (Nachi) that patches computers? If *everyone* did this, networks would be flooded with buggy worms and overworked admins → more harm than good → the rule fails → wrong.
* **Strengths:**
  * No full calculus for every choice — just follow good rules.
  * Exceptional cases don't overthrow rules (you keep the promise even when breaking it looks slightly better).
  * Solves *moral luck* by judging the rule's *typical* result.
  * Reduces bias — asks "is it OK for *everyone*?" not "is it OK for *me*?"
  * Appeals to a wide cross-section of people.
* **Weaknesses:**
  * Forces a *single scale* for utterly different consequences (sometimes even pricing a human life).
  * Ignores an *unjust distribution* of good (total happiness can be maximized while half the people get nothing).

#### Social Contract Theory

* **What it is:** Morality is the set of rules rational people agree to accept for mutual benefit, *on condition that others follow them too* — plus a government to enforce them (Hobbes, Rousseau, Rawls). Analyzed through **rights** (the flip side of everyone's duties).
* **Simplified:** We all give up a little freedom (I won't rob you) in exchange for security (you won't rob me). Rules + enforcement let everyone escape the "every man for himself" chaos.
* **Example:** Does store-owner Hao violate customers' rights by selling their purchase data? If customers have a *right* to expect transactions stay confidential, selling it without permission is wrong.
* **Strengths:**
  * Framed in the powerful, intuitive language of **rights**.
  * Grounded in a realistic view of human nature — people free-ride without enforcement (*tragedy of the commons*).
  * Explains when civil disobedience is justified (breaking *unjust* laws, e.g., the segregation sit-ins).
* **Weaknesses:**
  * "None of us actually signed it" (reply: it's *hypothetical*, not a historical contract).
  * Some actions can be characterized in more than one way.
  * Can't resolve *conflicting rights* (e.g., abortion — mother's liberty vs. fetus's life).
  * May be unjust to those unable to uphold their side of the contract.

#### Virtue Ethics

* **What it is:** A right action is what a *virtuous person, acting in character, would do* in the same situation. Focuses on the **agent**, not the act. Virtues (honesty, courage) are deep-seated habits built by practice, each sitting at the **golden mean** between two vices.
* **Simplified:** Don't ask "what's the right action?" — ask "what would a *good person* do here?" Become that person through habit.
* **Example:** Should Josh honestly report that his grieving friend Reggie slacked on the project? He weighs honesty vs. loyalty vs. compassion — and decides to tell the truth *while* owning his own earlier failures. It's about character, not a formula.
* **Strengths:**
  * Often simpler and more natural than reasoning about rules/rights/consequences.
  * Allows morally relevant **partiality** (parents *should* favor their own children).
  * Recognizes moral skill **develops over time** (learn from role models).
  * Takes **emotions** seriously (a good person's feelings align with their actions).
* **Weaknesses:**
  * People disagree on what "flourishing" and the virtues are, so it's less decisive than theories with universal rules.
  * Can't guide **government policy** (set by institutions, not virtuous individuals).
  * Complicates **blame** (if character comes from upbringing outside your control, how responsible are you?).

**Bottom line:** Each theory holds one valuable insight — utilitarians: consequences matter; social contract: protecting rights benefits all; virtue ethics: trust a good person to do right (Kant: every human is equally valuable). None is perfect, so weigh duties *and* rights *and* consequences *and* virtues — agreement across them means confidence; disagreement is where the hard, interesting cases live. **2.12** applies this: you have a *prima facie* duty to obey a just law unless a strong overriding moral obligation applies.
